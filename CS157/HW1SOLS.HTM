    (a) "It is impossible to enjoy idling thoroughly unless one has plenty of work to do" -Jerome K. Jerome
    p = "one has plenty of work to do"
    i = "It is impossible to enjoy idling thoroughly"

    ~p => i
    Explanation:  i is true.  However, when the p condition is met, there is no longer any gaurantee of i being true.  When p is false, i must be true.  In natural language the implication is sometimes that if the p condition is met, then i necesarily doesn't happen.  This is an inference and is not a precise interpretation of the meaning of unless.
    From Webster's Dictionary:
    unless \Un*less"\, conj. [Formerly, onles, onlesse, onlesse that, that is, in less, in a less case. See On, and Less.] Upon
    any less condition than (the fact or thing stated in the sentence or clause which follows); if not; supposing that not; if it be not; were it not that; except; as, we shall fail unless we are industrious.

    (b) Completing cs157 requires you are nice to Eitan, Danny, and Zoë, and not Professor Genesereth.
    c = "completing cs157"
    e = "you are nice to Eitan"
    d = "you are nice to Danny"
    z = "you are nice to Zoë"

    In Websters Dictionary

    5. Expressivity of Boolean Functions (7 points)  Graded by Zoe Abrams

    All propositional sentences can be expressed in clausal form (i.e. using the 2-arity operators '&' and '|' and the unary operator '~'). In fact, all propositional sentences can be expressed with even less: a single 2-arity operator. We'll call this operator '#'.

    Fill out the truth table for this operator below.
    (Note that a 2-arity operator can act as a unary operator by using a single proposition as both its operands.) 

    NOR
        p   |   q   |   p # q    | p # p  | q # q | (p#p) # (q#q)
  -----------------------------------------------------------
    T   |   T   |     F      |    F   |   F   |    T
    T   |   F   |     F      |    F   |   T   |    F
    F   |   T   |     F      |    T   |   F   |    F
    F   |   F   |     T      |    T   |   T   |    F
    can express all propositional sentences.  To see this,  p # p is equivalent to ~p (NOT).   Also, ~p # ~q is equivalent to p & q (AND).  We only need to express the '~' and '&' operators to write all sentences.  This is because, from clausal form, we know we only need '&', '~', and '|'.  By DeMorgan's laws, (x | y) <=> ~(~x & ~y) and (x & y) <=> ~(~x | ~y) so only one of '&' and '|' are needed.

    NAND also works for similar reasons.

    9. Proof Methods (18 points) Graded by Eitan Katznelson

    1. ~t => p & ~s
    2. r => s
    3. p & ~r => q & v
    (a) Prove ~t => q using only the premises above as well as the deduction theorem, Modus Ponens, Modus Tollens, &-Elimination, &-Introduction, Implication Introduction, and Conflict Realization; note that you may not need to use all of these.

    Solution: 

            1. ~t => p & ~s                         Premise
        2. r => s                               Premise
        3. p & ~r => q & v                      Premise
        4. ~t                                   Deduction Theorem
        5. p & ~s                               MP 1, 4
        6. ~s                                   AE 5
        7. p                                    AE 5
        8. ~r                                   MT 2, 6
        9. p & ~r                               AI 7, 8
        10. q & v                               MP 3, 9
        11. q                                   AE 10


    (b) Give a formal proof of ~t => q, using only resolution.

    Solution: 

       First, we convert the premises and the negation of the goal to
   clausal form:

          (~t => (p & ~s))
        I (~~t | (p & ~s))
        N (t | (p & ~s))
        D ((t | p) & (t | ~s))
        O {t, p}
          {t, ~s}


          (r => s)
        I (~r | s)
        N 
        D
        O {~r, s}


          (p & ~r => q & v)
        I ~(p & ~r)|(q & v) 
        N (~p | ~~r)|(q & v)
          (~p | r)|(q & v)
        D ((~p | r) | q) & ((~p | r) | v)
        
        O {~p, r, q}
          {~p, r, v}
        
         
          (~(~t => q))
        I (~(~~t | q))
        N (~(t | q))
          (~t & ~q)
        D
        O {~t}
          {~q}

    We then use these clauses to infer the empty clause via
    Propositional Resolution:

        1. {t,p}                Premise
        2. {t,~s}               Premise
        3. {~r,s}               Premise
        4. {~p, r, q}           Premise
        5. {~t}                 Negated Goal
        6. {~q}                 Negated Goal
        7. {p}                  1, 5
        8. {~s}                 2, 5
        9. {~r}                 3, 8
        10. {r, q}              4, 7
        11. {q}                 10, 9
        12. {}                  6, 11


    (c) Explain why you would not want to use a truth table to solve this problem.

    Solution: 

      The truth tables would be fairly large, 2^6 = 64 rows in fact, making

    10. Intelligent, Exercising Dogs? (20 points)  Graded by Danny Chai

    To solve this problem, you are free to use any formal method discussed in the lectures so far (including the truth table method). However, you are required to develop and to explicitly define your own logical constants; you are also required to write down, using these constants consistently, all the premises from the text that you choose to use in your proofs. Note that to answer the questions, it may not be necessary to encode all the information provided for you in the text; make sure that you do not mention in your solutions any premises that you do not use in the proofs.

    You've decided to buy a pair of Taibos, the new exercising robot dogs. Here is some of the information you learn from the instruction manual:

    • One Taibo is silver, the other is black.
    • The silver Taibo specializes in aerobics; the black Taibo specializes in jogging.
    • The Taibos are always in exactly one of 3 modes: ready, sleeping and off. They have also been set up so that they are always in the same mode (e.g. if you turn one on, the other one also turns on).
    • Each Taibo has a light that shines a different color depending on the mode. In ready mode, the light is green. In sleeping mode, the light is yellow. In off mode, the light is red.
    • If a Taibo runs out of power, it automatically turns off.
    • If the black Taibo jogs for 5 hours, it runs out of power.
    • If you clap twice when the silver Taibo is wagging its tail, it will roll over.
    • If the silver Taibo is in ready mode and is in the same room as its owner, it will start wagging its tail.
    • If a Taibo is not in the same room as its owner it will go into sleep mode.
    (a)  Suppose you've allowed your black Taibo to jog for 5 hours. Show that it follows from the data above that after these 5 hours, the silver Taibo will not be in ready mode.

    (b)  If you clap twice and the silver Taibo does not roll over, is the silver Taibo's light green? (Show that your answer follows from the data above.)

    Solution:

     Here are some example logical constants:

    sReady= "the silver Taibo is ready"
    sReady= "the silver Taibo is sleeping"
    bOff= "the black Taibo is off"
    sOff= "the silver Taibo is off"
    sGreen= "the silver Taibo's light is green"
    bNoPower= "the black Taibo runs out of power"
    bJog5Hours= "the black Taibo jogs for 5 hours"
    clapTwice= "the owner (you) clap twice"
    sWaggingTail= "the silver Taibo is wagging its tail"
    sRollOver= "the silver Tiabo rolls over"
    sSameRoom= "the silver Taibo is in the same room as the owner"
    We can translate some of the information from the text of the problem into the following logical sentences. Note that while we do not need to use all the information from each piece of information given (each bullet point), we cannot combine information from two bullet points to get a premise. 
     R1. bOff => sOff
 R2. sOff => ~sReady
 R3. sSleep => ~sReady
 R4. ~sReady => ~sGreen
 R5. bNoPower => bOff
 R6. bJog5Hours => bNoPower
 R7. clapTwice & sWaggingTail => sRollOver
 R8. sReady & sSameRoom => sWaggingTail
 R9. ~sSameRoom => sSleep
    Let us prove both (a) and (b) using resolution (since we are allowed to use any legal method). By applying INDO to the rules above, we obtain the following clauses: 
     1. {~bOff, sOff}                           From R1
 2. {~sOff, ~sReady}                        From R2
 3. {~sSleep, ~sReady}                      From R3
 4. {sReady, ~sGreen}                       From R4
 5. {~bNoPower, bOff}                       From R5
 6. {~bJog5Hours, bNoPower}                 From R6
 7. {~clapTwice, ~sWaggingTail, sRollOver}  From R7
 8. {~sReady, ~sSameRoom, sWaggingTail}     From R8
 9. {sSameRoom, sSleep}                     From R9
    Part a:
    The additional premise here is:
     
      
     10. {bJogged5Hours}                   Premise for 10a
    The negated goal is: 
     11. {sReady}                          Negated goal for 10a
    Running resolution: 
     12. {~sOff}                           Resolution: 2,11
 13. {~bOff}                           Resolution: 1,12
 14. {~bNoPower}                       Resolution: 5,13
 15. {~bJog5Hours}                     Resolution: 6,14
 16. {}                                Resolution: 10,15
    We've reached the empty clause, so the proof is complete.

    Part b:
    The additional premises here are:
     
      

     17. {clapTwice}                       Premise for 10b
 18. {~sRollOver}                      Premise for 10b
    We'll prove the silver Taibo's light is not green. Then the negated goal is: 
     19. {sGreen}                          Negated goal for 10b
    Running resolution: 
     20. {sReady}                          Resolution: 4,19
 21. {~sSameRoom, sWaggingTail}        Resolution: 8,20
 22. {~clapTwice, ~sWaggingTail}       Resolution: 7,18
 23. {~sWaggingTail}                   Resolution: 17,22
 24. {~sSameRoom}                      Resolution: 21,23
 25. {sSleep}                          Resolution: 9,24
 26. {~sReady}                         Resolution: 3,25
 27. {}                                Resolution: 20,26
    We've reached the empty clause, so the proof is complete.
